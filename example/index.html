Bauhaus

1919–1933

The Bauhaus school, founded by Walter Gropius in Weimar, Germany, unified fine art, craft, and technology under one pedagogical roof. Its core principle — form follows function — demanded that every visual decision serve a purpose. Faculty including Moholy-Nagy, Kandinsky, and Klee developed a foundation curriculum (Vorkurs) emphasizing geometric abstraction, primary colors, and modular composition. The school's output — from typography to furniture to architecture — established the blueprint for modern design education. When the Nazis closed it in 1933, its diaspora seeded modernist thinking worldwide.

Vorkurs geometric abstraction form follows function modular grid sans-serif typography

Examples: MoMA — Bauhaus Collection · MoMA — Walter Gropius

Not to be confused with Art Deco, because while both emerged in the early 20th century, Art Deco embraced ornamentation and luxury, while Bauhaus stripped design to functional essentials.

Swiss Style

International Typographic Style · 1950s–1970s

The International Typographic Style, born in Switzerland in the 1950s, codified Bauhaus principles into a systematic methodology. Josef Müller-Brockmann and Armin Hofmann championed the mathematical grid, asymmetric layouts, and objective photography over illustration. Akzidenz-Grotesk and later Helvetica became its signature typefaces — neutral, legible, universal. The style's insistence on clarity and visual hierarchy over self-expression made it the default language of corporate identity, wayfinding systems, and scientific communication for decades.

mathematical grid Helvetica asymmetric layout objective photography visual hierarchy

Examples: MoMA — Swiss Poster Design · MoMA — Typographic Composition

Not to be confused with Minimalism, because Swiss Style uses systematic grids and typographic rules to organize complex information clearly, while Minimalism reduces content itself to the fewest possible elements.

New Wave Typography

1970s–1980s

New Wave typography, pioneered by Wolfgang Weingart at the Basel School of Design, deliberately fractured the Swiss grid that his own teachers had perfected. Working from within the tradition, Weingart layered type, broke column rules, and introduced collage and compositional tension into what had been pristine compositions. His student April Greiman extended these experiments into early digital tools, merging bitmap imagery and overlapping grids. New Wave proved that systematic training could fuel rebellion — the designers who broke the rules understood them deeply first.

deconstructed grid layered typography bitmap imagery post-modernism compositional tension

Examples: Cooper Hewitt — April Greiman · MoMA — Wolfgang Weingart

Not to be confused with Grunge typography (David Carson's era), because New Wave emerged from formal Swiss training and maintained underlying structure, while Grunge rejected legibility itself as a design value.
